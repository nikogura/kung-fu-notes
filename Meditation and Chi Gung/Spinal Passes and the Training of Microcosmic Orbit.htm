Xiao Zhou Tian - variously known as Microcosmic orbit, Small Heaven circulation, Small Circulation, Turning the Falun wheel etc.: the circulation of Qi along DuMai-RenMai (Governing and Conception vessels respectively). Essentials of practice: within a selected posture, gather Qi to DanTian. When it has condensed and is moving like a warm stream, guide/ follow the Qi with your mind as you relax and allow it to. The path is down from DanTian to Huiyin, along Dumai upwards through the points of MingMen, Jiajo, Daizhui, Yuzhen, and BaiHui, then downwards through Shenting, Zuqiao, passing the nose and throat, down to Tanzhong and then back to DanTian. This is known as the Fire path and is represented in illustration to the right. The exact reverse of this is known as the Water path.

There is a third path known as the Wind path (shown to the left). This path runs from DanTian, up along the TaiJi pole (physiologically the gray matter at the center of the spine, in TCM terms, through the Marrow of the Spine), over the falix celebri at the base of the brain, and entering into Dumai at the roof of the mouth-tongue junction. The general effects of the exercises are to open up and balance the flow along Dumai and Renmai, and thus between Yin and Yang. The Daoist Alchemical model stresses the function of mixing of Yang Fire (from the Heart and Middle Dantian) and Yin Water (from the Kidney) and hence aiding the transformation of Jing into Shen. Consistent practice of the exercise will benefit the body's WeiQi (defensive Qi field), strengthening immune function and increasing sensitivity and interaction with one's environment. If TaiJi, or similar internal art, is practiced, the exercise will increase the ability to listen to (and understand) the opponent/ partner and to stick-follow. Yu Gongbao expresses it like this:

"Yin and Yang propel, attract and mingle with eachother, and in turn cause changes in nature. Sustain Jing and Qi to nourish the body, and cultivate ZhenJi (the moment when the prenatal and postnatal Qi combines into one) in conformity with the law of nature and your health will improve a lot."

The specific functions of the exercises are:

Fire: Yang in nature, purifies and cleanses the body's Qi, purges toxic/ pathogenic Qi

Water: Yin in nature, regulates and charges Shen (perceptional consciousness)

Wind: increases intuitive/ spiritual consciousness, regulates glandular and endocrine system (the pathway passes through the pineal and pituitary glands).


The Training of Microcosmic Circulation

Essential Pre-Requsites for Practice

Microcosmic Circulation is not a beginner's exercise. It requires a substantial supply of Qi in DanTian and experience with feeling Qi move and flow, as well as steady concentration and practiced use of intention. Any student not possessing these should, under no circumstances, attempt Microcosmic circulation. Please note the following from Liu Housheng and Lou Peiyu:

"this exercise is not suitable for beginners. Only when they have had considerable practice, and developed sufficient internal Qi can they practice this exercise. When the Qi starts functioning you should let it go naturally. Never lead it with much effort nor use the mind too early, otherwise deviations will occur."

There are two methods, depending on style of teacher and ability/ suitability of student.

Method 1: Practice Patiently

as already mentioned, Microcosmic circulation will occur when DanTian fills with Qi, and subsequent Qi overflows up Dumai. As such, it is not necessary to train any special methods: consistent right practice of a static posture and a little patience ensure a result. A sitting or standing posture are preferred. Breathing will be deep and natural or reverse (paradoxical) depending on individual exercise and style of teaching.

Regulate the breathing to get it even and smooth. Relax the body and let the mind rest on DanTian. The mind should follow the Qi, but should neither force nor ignore any sensations of the flow or gathering of Qi. Thus be an interested observer and note what is happening; do not be a conscious and forceful participant. After a certain time of practice (strongly dependent on experience, regularity etc.), the DanTian will become substantial with Qi. This feeling will increase, and Qi will start to move out from the DanTian like a stream of hot air or water. The direction of movement should be down to the coccyx, up DuMai, passing over and into RenMai and back to DanTian. Some may feel the Qi divide into two after Shenting, moving down past the eyes and along the cheek bones before uniting again within the mouth. Here it passes the top of the tongue and enters Renmai. The student should not be worried if the full path does not open up immediately on feeling Qi move up Dumai. This is natural, and to be expected. If the student experiences persistent problems where Qi flows up to a specific point and stops as though it meets a dam or blockage, they should consult their teacher and maybe read the section on the Spinal Passes below.

All this will happen naturally if sufficient time and patience is allowed. Sometimes there is need to give the Qi a small prompt to begin, in which case the principle of 'focusing attention merely when Qi has not started to move, and leading Qi to circulate when it is about to move' should be followed. At no time should the flow of Qi be forced, or intention be too strong and insistent less deviations occur.

Method 2: Practice Systematically

For those students who require, or those teachers who prefer, a more systematic approach, the following, or something similar, is recommended as long as caution is followed. A systematic approach is often necessitated for those who have not achieved quiescence, or whose patience may fail, or who may fall into the trap so that, as no new exercise has been introduced, they cease to practice sincerely. In short, it is the easier method, but saves no time and greater caution is indicated in its practice. In particular, the supervision of an experienced QiGong practitioner is advocated to ensure that progression to the next stage is allowed only when the student is ready, and that the flow of Qi is unforced by the intention of an overeager mind. In this way, deviations will be avoided.

Exercise 1: Turning the TaiJi Ball (or Rotating the DanTian)

When the student is fully relaxed in the chosen posture (a natural standing or sitting position is best), and the breathing is slow, unforced, and follows the path straight from nose to DanTian, the exercise should be introduced.

On the inhale, the Huiyin should be raised to create a dynamic tension. The inhale should be enough to form the DanTian into a ball, but the student should not force themselves to breath too deeply. Breathing may be regular, or a slight pause may be introduced when the breath is held after the inhale. Under no circumstances should this be awkward or uncomfortable. The ball will tend to turn backward and upward as shown to the left. The first sensation will be experienced by one month of practice. Further practice will bring increased degree of rotation, and then an increased number of rotations per breath cycle. The exhale should be relaxed and the Huiyin dropped.

An alternative method requires that, after the inhale with Huiyin raising up, Qi should be guided with gentle intention in a triangle from Huiyin to MingMen to QiHai. This should be attempted slowly, evenly, naturally, and softly. In only a little time, the student will feel the points heating up, and a sensation of Qi flow will be achieved some time later.

 

Exercise 2: Spinal Column Path

During this article, we shall take the Lion Holds Ball posture as an illustration. This is achieved by standing with legs shoulder width apart, a slight bend at the knees, arms held out at about shoulder height and bent as though clasping a large ball to the chest. It is also known as Standing post, or Standing Like a Tree.
On the inhale, raise the Huiyin and guide Qi to the coccyx. From here, guide the Qi to flow up the spine (the dorsal surface). At the most basic level, the Qi should rise up to the shoulder blades. On the exhale, Qi should be guided down the arms and held in circulation within the ball. When it is time to finish the exercise, the student should guide the stored Qi to flow from within the posture back to DanTian. Qi flow should be calm and stabilized before completion of the exercise. This is best achieved by reverting to the natural posture held in the exercise before, hands either hanging loosely or held infront of DanTian, mind on DanTian, and guide any Qi which is still moving back to its source in DanTian. When all feelings have settled down, the exercise has ended. A more advanced level is to guide the Qi to the crown of the head (the point BaiHui) on the inhale.

On the exhale, the direction of flow is reversed from crown to Dantian to nose. On alternate inhales, Qi should be directed from the crown along two paths. One reverses the flow to the shoulder blades from where it is passed through the arms and into the posture as immediately above. The second path continues down from the crown, along the front of the head, and out of the nose. It is this, more advanced stage of the exercise, that is illustrated in the images above left, and to the right.

Exercise 3: Exhale and Inhale Through the Soles of the Feet

On the inhale, Qi is brought through the nose and drawn up through the legs from YongQuan (Kidney 1) situated on the sole of the foot. The exhale alternates between a simple reversal of this flow, and storage in the posture of Holding Ball. If the student finds it difficult to open up the path through the legs, they may focus on achieving this in coordination with the breathing without any conscious flow up through the spine. Proceed as follows. Perform Turning the TaiJi Ball exercise. Note that on the inhale, the direction of rotation is from front to back, from bottom to up. This reverses on the exhale, thus pointing directly down the legs. Guide the Qi through the legs to YongQuan on the exhale, and reverse the direction for the inhale. The student will find this easier to accomplish that the full exercise. Once the student is comfortable with this path (this should occur very quickly on feeling the path open, though it may take some time to open the path itself), they should proceed to the exercise proper.

 

 

Exercise 4: Microcosmic Circulation

This follows on quite naturally from the preceding exercise. There are numerous variations on this final step, and I consider here only the simplest. On the inhale, raise Huiyin and lead Qi to the DanTian. After the inhale is complete, pause with gently held breath. During this pause, lead the Qi up the spine, past the crown, down to the nose tip, and finally into Renmai and return to DanTian. Relax the body with the exhale. If this exercise is attempted at the correct time, and after patient practice of the other exercises, Qi should surge up Dumai like a strong warm stream, and trickle easily down Renmai like condensing rain. This is not the end of the practice however. Microcosmic circulation must be practiced until it occurs naturally, and without the conscious involvement of the practitioner. The path will be open, and Qi will flow along it in everyday life as well as during QiGong practice. By this stage, the practitioner has reached the same stage as someone who has chosen or been instructed in the patient methods of Method 1. They are both different in their approach, but they reach the same end.

To aid the student in their search for Microcosmic circulation, suitable dynamic forms should be taught alongside the static practice. The purpose of these forms is to relax and open the spine, nourish DanTian, ease passage through the spinal passes, and to open up and dredge the two vessels. The student need not be aware of this purpose, they only need practice the forms. Suitable exercises will be presented in the following section: a discussion on the Spinal passes, and how to release and open them if blocked.


QiGong Deviations and the Spinal Passes

It would be entirely normal for a person to have restricted flow of Qi through one or even all of the passes. A student should be patient as to when to begin Xiao Zhou Tian. In particular, there should be sufficient supply of DanTian Qi, and the spine should be flexible and strong. Should any pass be restricted, the student must not be overanxious as deviations may occur. The student should be reassured that gentle and patient practice inevitably leads to a natural opening of all of the passes, and Microcosmic orbit will be achieved.

If the student's overanxiousness leads to increasing restriction, stasis of Qi and Blood, or even damage to the channel and separation of Yin and Yang, an experienced QiGong practitioner or Master should be sought. They will ease the blockage by the methods described below, or other legitimate techniques. It is to be remembered that it is the malpractice of QiGong exercises, and not the exercises themselves, that is the cause of deviations.

The existence of the passes/ gates refers to specific points along Dumai through which it can be difficult for Qi to pass. To experience, maintain, and develop Microcosmic Orbit, it is essential that these passes are open, and that they continue so. If any of the passes are blocked, the flow of Qi will be hindered and, in severe cases, Qi and Blood will stagnate around the point. Remai and Dumai will then be in a state of disconnection. Blockages at the points will be felt as aching pain, feeling that there is something building up and putting pressure on the point from below, feeling of dense lump, constriction, feeling of emptiness above the point, etc. Sources differ as to the number of the passes: some say five, others three. They are given on the diagram to the left:


BaiHui: cranial pass

YuZhen: occipital pass

 

LuLu: upper spinal pass, between the shoulders



JiaJi: Mingmen, and two points laterally


Weilu: lower spinal pass or coccyx pass

 

General Treatment (TuiNa, Jing point therapy, External-QiGong/ Outgoing-Qi)

Principles: Dredge the Meridians, activate Collaterals, promote Qi and Blood flow, resolve Stasis, rectify joints, ease tendons

Methods: Push the back with pinching and grasping, Deep-Press the spine with kneading and revolving, Pull the waist with jerking and dragging, Strengthen the waist by rolling and rubbing, Pluck Collaterals with knocking and scratching.

If symptoms are combined with Lumbago: Stimulate MingMen, simultaneously guide Qi to Bl-40. Emit Qi into Bl-18, guide Qi to flow along channel and down legs. Deep press bent legs to tug the waist, TaiJi round rub the abdomen. Rectify the back and waist. Jerk and drag to relax the waist.

If symptoms are combined with pain and swelling of DaZhui (Big Vertebra): Press and kneed DaZhui, FengFu, FengChi, and JiZhong (Du-14, 16, 20, 6), pat along DuMai and Bladder channel and/or emit and lead Qi with vibrating flat palm gesture and pushing-dragging. Grasp the swelling and tug gently up and down.

Specific Treatment Prescribed Per Pass:

Weilu: patient directs intention to point, tongue presses against the upper palate, inhale deeply, raise Huiyin and gently contract abdomen. Massage at MingMen, direct Qi up DuMai.

JiaJi: extend Qi into MingMen, lead Qi up DuMai with the other hand (using pushing-pulling manipulations). Rectify waist and spine.

LuLu: extend Qi into the pass, lead QI up DuMai with the other hand (using pushing-pulling). Rectify spine and shoulder.

YuZhen: patient closes eyes, looks upward whilst gently concentrating on BaiHui. They should imagine the blockage as a cube of ice, steadily melting. The molten water turns into steam, and the vapor travels up DuMai, and down Renmai.

BaiHui: as above.

Should more significant deviations be experienced, all practice of the exercises must cease until an experienced practitioner or Master can be consulted. Amongst the more common deviations are:

  • Difficulty breathing, shallow or labored breathing, distress: generally due to excessive concentration on the breathing, or too deep breathing restricting oxygen supply. May also accompany other deviations. The body naturally compensates by changing the breathing to a deviation pattern. Relax and carry out natural, relaxed breathing and symptoms will disappear.

  • Headache, pulsing head, feeling of distention in the head and neck: due to always concentrating on Qi flow up to BauHui, and neglecting flow back to DanTian. Result is dense, stagnated Qi in the head. Easily prevented by ensuring flow is too and from DanTian, and that the exercise is ended by all Qi returning to source.

  • Dizziness, light headedness, syncope: disturbed and interrupted Qi flow, sudden rushing of Qi to the head. Caused by insufficient relaxed concentration, practicing when preoccupied or not properly warmed up and focused, lack of energetic grounding to the Earth. Guide Qi to DanTian, self-massage GB-21, ST-36 and K-1. Develop better easy intention (relaxed focus, concentration) and grounding before returning to exercise.

  • Mild pain or discomfort in the knees during standing posture: this is a normal pattern, and should lessen and disappear with practice. Severe or stabbing pain, pain lasting beyond exercise etc. is a deviation. Specific exercises will be recommended to strengthen the knees before returning to standing practice. It is important to ensure that all QiGong exercises obey the rules for correct posture (see elsewhere on the site).

  • Experience of lights or sounds (hallucination) during practice: this is a normal pattern and most people will experience it at some stage in their practice. Care should be taken not to become fixated by these experiences, or to willingly seek them out at the expense of what is happening in this practice session, and this moment. This behavior is a psychological deviation and can lead to disconnection with reality. Such a person should be instructed only under the closest supervision of a senior Master.

  • Need to pass wind, grumbling stomach: these are normal patterns, and will lessen and disappear with some little practice. They represent an increased flow of Qi to the bowels which respond by functioning at increased efficiency. Consistent practice keeps the efficient state permanently.

  • No feeling: the student should not expect to master the exercise in a day.

  • Feeling warm, sweating: due to increased WeiQi circulation. Normal if moderate. Care should be taken to keep dry and not catch chill after the exercise is completed. To this end, always carry an extract jacket etc., and never practice in or near a draft or in a damp environment.

  • Feeling cold, chilled: practice should be finished by guiding Qi to DanTian. Drink some warm fluids, perform gentle self-massage, and resume practice the next day.


Dynamic Exercises for the Treatment and Prevention of Blockage at the Spinal Passes

The following are a few examples from amongst a huge variety of suitable exercises. Those given have specific therapeutic benefits to the back and spine. However, students should not neglect the information stored within their own systems such as TaiJi etc. Where necessary their instructor should be consulted. For example, in the BaGuaZhang system taught by Park Bok Nam, the following exercises would have therapeutic effect upon the spinal passes: throwing palm, hiding flower under leaf, Fan Zhang, and dragon back (advanced/ difficult - probably better as prevention than cure). See the bibliography for books in which these may be found. There are numerous other examples (the author is not advocating one system over another)

Expanding and Contracting the Rings

Refers to the expansion and contraction experienced by the spinal vertebra during this exercise. Exercise will benefit the back, spine, coordination, balance, and cause Yang to descend and Yin to ascend. Begin the exercise from a right Bow Stance (weight 70% on the rear foot) hands positioned in front of the lower DanTian, fingers curled easily inwards. Both hands raise upward and stretch forward in a smooth arc. When initialy practising the exerice, the student should just concentrate on integrating the breathing with the movement and not falling forward off-balance. Inhale with the movement up close to the body, exhale as you stretch forward. The full exercise is practised as follows: when extending and stretching, visualize Yin Qi from the Earth traveling up the rear leg, through the spine, up Governing Vessel to BaiHui, extending down to YinTang, and both LaoGong points in the palms. While pushing outwards from the body, use intention to connect with Yang Qi from Heaven or an object on the horizon. As both hands withdraw, shift the weight back to the rear leg (it has come forward as the hands have moved forward), contracting the spinal column. Each repetition is completed by shifting back to the original position and drawing Yin Qi up from YongQuan to DanTian. Repeat nine times, then reverse direction for another nine (the breathing also changes such that one inhales when stretching forward and exhales when pressing downward). This time, visualize Yang Qi descending from Heaven, traveling from BaiHui, down the Conception Vessel and out of the front foot, extending deep into the earth. As the weight shifts back, use intention to draw Qi from the Heavens into the body (down to DanTian) through the head and hands. An important part of the exercise is the shifting of the hips to ensure that the spine is stretched without causing the balance to topple. This is acheived such that the hands acheive their furthest psoition forward when approximately level with the shoulders (they may be just below). At this stage, the movement backwards should start from the hips - the student should not just fall forward and into the front hip.

Repeat on the other side of the body. Those practicing the exercise for a blocked spinal pass will want to carry out the exercise without the use of intention (focus on DanTian) until the stasis begins to dissolve. Afterwards, perform the exercise as above.

Rolling the ball with both palms

Regulates Yin and Yang, opens Collaterals along Microcosmic Orbit, stimulates nerves in the extremities. Purges and regulates the emotions in the Heart and Yellow Court, regulates descending of Yin and ascending of Yang if in excess.

Begin from a relaxed standing posture. Raise both arms up infront of the shoulders and shake vigorously. Draw Qi from Heaven and up from Earth, and form a ball in front of the chest (Middle DanTian area). It should be possible to feel some sort of resistance to the hands pressing inwards as well as feelings of heat, buzzing, aching, fullness etc.

When you can feel the sensation of the ball between your hands, visualize this ball, and the ball at lower DanTian blending and mixing into one (this presumes you can feel your DanTian as such - most begginers start out without an awareness of DanTian, which slowly changes to a feeling of something about the size of a wallnut, and grows from there). This allows for rotatation both internally and eternally. Begin rolling the ball, keeping intention focused at LaoGong on both palms. As you roll that ball, it grows between the hands. The rolling is carried out by hands opposite eachother (one hand up, the other down), eighteen times clockwise, eighteen times anti-clockwise. After the last rotation, relax back into a natural standing posture.

This exercise is not really all that dynamic, but the degree of Qi movement generated is considerable. Beginners may find it difficult to blend the ball held between the hands and DanTian. "Expanding" both outwards until they first touch, and then letting the two mix together may help - this makes use of the intention (Yi) and should not be carried out until some degree of quiescence (calm focus) is acheived.

 

Water Flowing on a High Mountain

Exercise will dispel Qi stasis in the back and neck muscles. Care must be taken not to strain or tense the muscles, or headaches and a tight and painful neck may result. It forms part of a history of Taoist exercises going back 3000 years (it is - probably - found on the DaoYin Tu discovered in a Tomb dating from 168BC near ChangSha, Hunan province). There are many more of such exercises - the next two areincluded for illustrative purposes, as well as being effective in their own right.

Sit upright with both legs straightened out, heels resting on the floor, feet pointing upwards. Raise your hands and keep their fingers interlocked overhead. Push hands up with all of the body (as though holding a heavy stone between the hands - you should feel the spine stretch upwards. It is important to avoid raising and tensing the shoulders and neck). Exhale and place hands on top of the head. Repeat nine times. It is important to end the exercise properly - any method may be chosen as long as it guides intention and Qi back to the Dantian and leaves the mind feeling as though it were resting comfortably in the body. No sensation of tightness in the neck or head should occur during or after this exercise. Some gentle massaging or rubbing of the DanTian and neck may be done if you so wish.

 

Looking Up at Heaven and Down at Earth

Will limber up muscles and joints, ensure the smooth flow of Blood and Qi, aid against Lumbago, tightness of the chest, dyspnea, and arthrosis.

Stand at ease with feet shoulder-width apart and hands clenched into a loose fist. Slowly bend over and push the fists down as far as you can making sure you are opening up through the lower back and shoulders and not closing off these areas or forcing the movement. Straighten and raise the fists overhead as the spine striaghtens back to its normal position. Inhale before you bend down, hold the breath whilst you are bent over, and exhale as you straighten out. Return to the beginning and repeat for a total of nine times.

Care should be taken when holding the breath (particularly if treating tight chest or dyspnea). At no stage should you feel uncomfortable, out of breath, or straining to breathe. In all of these exercises, it is important that the body is opening up its joints and not locking into or closing down. Frequent mistakes are to actually tense up the lower back and hips when performing these exercises - the exact opposite of what is intended. It is therefore important to develop an honest attitude to practice - one should not deceive oneself that one is doing the exercise correctly if you can still feel tension or tightness in the posture or breathing.

One should use this bio-feedback to resolve the tensions and increase the accurateness with which one performs the exercise, the fundamentals of structure, and the observation of Liu He (Six Harmonies) when moving. In this manner, the exercises will aid the development of a lose spine and the induction of Microcosmic Orbit.
